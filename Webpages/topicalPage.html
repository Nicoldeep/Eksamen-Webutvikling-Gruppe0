Technology and society

The politics of infrastructure

We rely on the internet to get current day information which includes the days political news. Politicians use social media platforms to spread their ideology and to gain followers, but they don’t have total freedom to do so. There are consequences to using social media platforms because they have their own rules and regulations. And if a politician were to break any of those rules, they risk getting banned from the platform which would greatly affect their reach. Because we are so reliant on the internet, there would be major consequences if someone were to abuse the infrastructure. A current day example is Russia and the limitations they have put in place to quench information regarding the current war. They control the information that gets fed to the user, and thus affect the users’ political views. There have also been dictatorial shutdowns of the internet infrastructure of a whole country in order to prevent discussion and spread of information. We have all heard of the term “fake news” and how that affects political views, whether it be about vaccines or the distrust of politicians. In our agreeable opinion, to ensure safe consumption of information, one should use different medias, even outside of the internet so that the internet wouldn’t have as much of a consequence on our politics in case it all went berserk.

RRI (Responsible Research and innovation)

The statement “The person who invented the train invented the train crash” is great way of understanding the societal responsibilities innovators are involved in. We have a general understanding of consequences when something is being innovated, yet there will always be unintended consequences like climate changes and nuclear pollution. We believe development of technology requires care and good intentions after it has been developed for it to end up as good intentions. Example of Frankenstein mistake being not following up on his monster and caring about it (The breakthrough Institiute, 2012). Innovators have the responsibility to manage the work they do with ethical guidelines, understanding what impact it has on society and the risk it can cause. As an example, when developing an AI, innovators have the responsibility to follow up the technology, so it doesn’t run unattended. Worst case scenario, AI will try to kill humanity, or even worse, puppies. Despite being a seemingly high risk, the use of AI in a society will have an immense positive effect to societies all over the world. As long as the developers and innovators of AI do it in a way that the AI doesn't go crazy it will have a positive outcome. All in all, a great way of solving misunderstanding of consequences in a project is by innovators considering themselves as citizens and a part of society, because we’re all stuck on planet earth for now.

Big data and aritifical intelligence

How does algorithms play a part in your life

We start by explaining what our understanding of algorithms is, and so to tell by what our tiny brains understand is that it is a sequence of numeric variables playing a role for an outcome. In other words, algorithm is a recipe to a product, answer, or in our case recipe to solve a problem. Algorithms have a big role in our daily life, all kinds of machines function on it. Different algorithms for different “problems”. Our phone, pc at work, calculators when we aren’t smart enough to do the math with our brain etc. We mostly see the solved part of most algorithms, not the solving part. The reason for that is most human brains can’t fathom how complex algorithms work. Neither could we, how java actually works is not something we know. We might have an idea but not a full understanding. We write something and the algorithm solves it. Our main questions are then how do we make complex algorithms? Someone has to make them, right? Our initial guess is that all algorithms started small. Easy algorithms like one and one equals two, and someone put several algorithms together to make a complex one. In other words, if we understand this right, algorithms are something all humans can do, just not maybe understand?

Artifical Intelligence

We don’t know much about the health care industry. What we do know is that anytime we get sick, feel some sort of pain, or have any kind of symptoms for something, we go to the doctor to get a medical point of view. In most of our cases doctors have good guidance and experience on what might be our problem and prescribes us with a solution, sometimes being a drug. But what about the cases which aren’t the usual ones. We are talking about ER and spontaneous incidents of symptoms that can be caused by all kinds of health problems. Symptoms like tumor, internal bleeding, extreme coughing etc. These situations would be hard for a doctor to understand the underlying problem of the patient just by looking at them. Or in other words just by one symptom which could just be one of several symptoms to come. And yes, with common sense one can understand that more symptoms make it easier to know what causes the symptoms. Or if there have been past symptoms and past ER visits it will be in the patient journal. But there are millions of health problems in the book, so which one could it be? With machine learning or AI to help this all would be easier right? Not entirely. Yes, we believe it will help with narrowing cause of problems if the data the machine has is enough and right, but it can also narrow it too much down with little data of certain problems, or vice versa. Then there is the trusting part, if the variables machine has considered is the right ones (if the tests are correct). The main challenge we believe to be is, “when is the data enough to make medical decisions?”

The story of the scientist Frankenstein who created a monster that he lost control over is a powerful allegory for innovation gone wrong. In this essay, the philosopher Bruno Latour argues that Frankenstein's sin was not his attempt to create a human being, but his negligence of the creature he had created. He argues that innovation needs to be followed up with care for what has been developed, to avoid that technological development gets out of hand and to ensure beneficial outcomes of innovations. (The breakthrough Institiute, 2012)
